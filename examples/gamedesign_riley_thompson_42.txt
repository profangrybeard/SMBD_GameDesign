ACTIVITY 3: RULE HACKING (Req 4)
========================================
Rule Change 1:
  Prediction: Everyone will just throw rock all the time and games will be boring
  Result: First few rounds yes, but then people started faking out — one kid threw paper anyway as a joke and it got really funny
  Feeling: Less strategic, more silly. Like the game broke on purpose.
Rule Change 2:
  Prediction: Whoever throws second always wins because they see the other person's hand
  Result: Pretty much yes — but the person who waited too long got accused of cheating. We had to add a rule about how long you could delay.
  Feeling: Tense and a little annoying — the game stopped being about reading people and started being about waiting.
Rule Change 3:
  Prediction: Will make matches longer but more fair so one bad throw doesn't lose it
  Result: Yes — and the wild-card move (jazz hands = beats anything once per match) made it really exciting because you had to decide WHEN to use it
  Feeling: Way more strategy. People started bluffing about whether they'd already used the wild card.

ACTIVITY 4: DESIGN YOUR GAME (Req 5)
========================================
Vision: Dragon Hoard is a 2-4 player card game where each player is a dragon trying to build the biggest treasure pile. You play in 10 minutes and every game ends with a tense final turn where someone might steal everything.
Name: Dragon Hoard
Players: 2-4
Medium: Card game
Time: 10-15 min
Theme: Greedy dragons collecting shiny stuff
Goals: Push-your-luck, Tension
Core Loop:
  Step 1: Draw 2 cards from the hoard pile
  Step 2: Decide: keep them safe, or risk stealing from another dragon?
  Step 3: Play your action card face-down
  Step 4: All players flip — resolve thefts in age order
  Step 5: Score gems on cards still in your hoard, check if anyone hit 25
Rules: 1. You must draw at least 1 card per turn.
2. You can only steal from dragons with bigger hoards than you.
3. If two dragons steal from each other on the same turn, neither succeeds.
4. First to 25 gems triggers the final round.
5. Knight cards force you to discard your top card.
Resources: 60 cards: 30 treasure (gems), 15 action cards (steal/defend), 10 knights, 5 wild dragons. 4 player mats. Score tokens.

========================================
PLAYTEST LOG (Req 6)
========================================
Round 1:
  Worked: The pushing-your-luck part was fun — Sam laughed when he stole my whole stack
  Broken: First-player advantage was huge. Whoever went first won twice in a row.
  Changed: Whoever has the smallest hoard goes first each round
  Why: To balance out the lead — give the loser a chance to catch up
  Result: Way better. Games stayed close until the end.
Round 2:
  Worked: Knight cards added great panic moments. The reaction when one came out was the best part.
  Broken: Mom said the rules took 5 minutes to explain — too many phases per turn
  Changed: Combined steps 3 and 4 — everyone plays AND flips at the same time
  Why: Cut a step out. Less to remember = faster turns.
  Result: Game went from 20 min to 12 min. Still felt strategic.
Round 3:
  Worked: Bluffing! Asher faked having a knight for two rounds and we all believed him
  Broken: The wild-dragon cards were too strong — whoever got one usually won
  Changed: Wild dragons can only be played in the final round
  Why: Save the big swing for the climax instead of letting it decide things early
  Result: Made the final round HUGELY tense. This is the version I'm submitting.

========================================
BLIND TEST (Req 7)
========================================
Setup: Shuffle all 60 cards into one deck. Deal 3 cards face-down to each dragon as their starting hoard. Each player picks a dragon mat. Place score tokens in the middle. Smallest hoard goes first (random for round 1).
Play: On your turn: 1) Draw 2 cards from the deck. 2) Look at your hand. 3) Choose ONE action card (steal, defend, hoard) and place it face-down. 4) When everyone is ready, all players flip at once. Steals resolve youngest player first. Then count any gems revealed and add to your score.
Win: First player to score 25 gems triggers the final round. Everyone else gets one more turn. Highest score wins. Ties go to whoever has the fewest knight cards on their mat.
Misunderstood: They thought 'defend' protected forever, not just the current turn. We had to explain it 3 times.
Unexpected: One player stockpiled cards face-down without ever attacking — turtling. We didn't think anyone would do that.
Stuck: When two players both played 'steal' on each other. The rules say neither succeeds, but they assumed it would be a duel.
Fun Moment: Round 4 — when Asher pulled out a wild dragon at exactly 24 points and won. Whole table erupted.
Like Most: The bluffing. Card games where you have to read people are way more fun than just luck or just math. Dragon Hoard has both.
Change: Add a rules card players keep at their seat — explaining it is the slowest part. Also rename 'turtling' to a real strategy with a card name.

========================================
REFLECTION (Req 8b)
========================================
1: I learned that a game can be 'broken' in fun ways and not-fun ways. Rock-beats-everything was broken-funny. First-player advantage was broken-bad.
2: I learned that explaining rules out loud is the hardest part — even rules I wrote myself. Now I get why every game I own has a quick-reference card.
3: I learned that the FEELING of the game (the panic, the bluff, the laugh) matters more than the rules. The rules are just there to make those feelings happen.
